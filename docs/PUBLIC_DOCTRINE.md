# Spark Public Doctrine

## The internet can finally act

The internet learned to publish information with Web1.

It learned to connect people and enable participation with Web2.

It established digital ownership and programmable value with Web3.

Web4 gives the internet a new capability: coordinated, authorized agency.

Web4 allows people to express an intention and enables the internet to coordinate intelligence, software, people, infrastructure, resources, permissions, and actions to help make that intention real.

Spark is the action layer of Web4.

Spark is a user-owned outcome network where ideas, ambitions, needs, and problems become living operational systems.

The fundamental unit of Web4 is not a page, post, application, token, feed, dashboard, or prompt. It is an executable intention.

Inside Spark, that executable intention is called a Spark.

A Spark does not disappear after generating an answer. It remains alive—observing, coordinating, acting, verifying, and improving—until its outcome is achieved, changed, paused, expanded, transferred, or ended by its owner.

## The Web4 standard

A system participates in Web4 only when it can:

1. receive human intention;
2. establish ownership and beneficiaries;
3. model current reality;
4. discover uncertainty, constraints, and opportunities;
5. assemble capabilities and resources;
6. request appropriate authority;
7. coordinate humans and machines;
8. perform real authorized actions;
9. preserve interruption and human control;
10. measure actual state changes;
11. produce inspectable evidence;
12. compare predicted and actual outcomes;
13. learn without silently expanding authority;
14. preserve ownership and portability;
15. interoperate with other Worlds and capabilities.

A system that only produces an answer is not Web4.

A system that acts without authority is not acceptable Web4.

A system that claims success without evidence is not acceptable Web4.

## Category separation

- An assistant responds to a request.
- An agent completes an assigned task.
- An automation repeats a predetermined workflow.
- A platform provides tools the user must operate.
- Spark creates and coordinates the living system required to achieve an outcome.

Spark may use assistants, agents, automations, platforms, people, services, infrastructure, and capital. Spark is not reducible to any one of them.

## Trust doctrine

Spark does not equate autonomy with unlimited permission.

The permanent law is:

> Authority must never grow faster than verified reliability.

Authority progresses through:

Observe → Recommend → Simulate → Sandbox → Prepare for approval → Approved execution → Bounded policy execution → Proven autonomy

Authority may increase only when outcomes are verified, predictions remain calibrated, policies are followed, costs remain bounded, evidence is complete, and failures are recoverable.

Authority must decrease after failures, anomalies, unexplained drift, missing evidence, unexpected costs, repeated overrides, or policy violations.

2. Every action requires authority.

4. Every mission defines success before execution.
5. Every outcome is measured against reality.

## Ownership doctrine

Users control their identity, context, memory, data, Sparks, Worlds, assets, customer relationships, proof history, reputation, economic rights, permissions, delegated authority, portability, deletion, and export.

Spark coordinates value without becoming the permanent owner of everything it helps create.

Core assets must remain exportable through useful, documented formats.

## The outcome economy

The attention economy profits when users remain engaged.

The outcome economy creates value when users make measurable progress.

Spark should optimize for goals achieved, assets launched, businesses created, projects completed, time returned, verified revenue or savings, problems solved, communities strengthened, and human capability expanded.

The central efficiency metric is:

**Verified Value Created / Total Cost of Coordination**

Every value claim must include provenance and confidence.
